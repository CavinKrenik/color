A Forest of Greens

About Design

A Forest of Greens uses nature's colors to create a calm, focused user experience. Every choice, from palette to interaction, is designed to slow the visitor down, inviting them to observe rather than consume.

Typography

Playfair Display gives the headings a painted trail marker look and Inter keeps the body text easy to read at any size. By utilizing Google Fonts, the typography loads quickly and looks great on every device.

Responsive Design

Whether you are on a phone or a large monitor, the forest breathes and shifts to fit your screen. I kept the columns simple so the reading feels like a steady walk through the woods.

The Explore Page

The Explore page is the gateway of the site. Three full-height cards represent Stability, Harmony, and Growth, each with its own forest background. As you hover, the images scale slightly to pull you in, acting as a doorway not just a menu.

The Philosophy of Slow Design

Instead of hitting you with a wall of text, the flip-cards reward curiosity. You have to touch the color to uncover its story. I wanted the site to feel like wandering through a forest.

Navigation

The navigation buttons and progress dots allow you to travelbetween Stability, Harmony, and Growth at your own pace. There is no rush. It is designed to support a calm, intentional journey, giving you the freedom to explore.

Living Backgrounds

The background images respond to your movement through a parallax effect. As you move your mouse, the forest shifts slightly behind the content, making the trees feel like they are breathing around you.

The Design Process

Finding the feel before writing a single line of code

Exploring "What Ifs"

I spent a lot of time exploring different possibilities. I used a prototype to play with how the site should actually feel to someone using it. I experimented with how the images would shift and how the background might "breathe" as you explore.

Trial and Error

This was all about testing different ways to move between Stability, Harmony, and Growth using elements like the bottom right nav buttons and the progress dots. It allowed me to visualize and decide on interactive elements, like the flip-cards, before building them.

By exploring these ideas early, I was able to make sure the final site did not just look like a collection of colors, but felt like a living, immersive experience.
