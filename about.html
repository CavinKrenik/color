About

A Forest of Greens is a digital color study exploring the psychological and historical narrative of nature’s primary palette. This project serves as an interactive field guide, taking the viewer on a deliberate journey from the grounded permanence of the forest floor to the vibrant energy of new life.

The Narrative Journey

Stability

Representing Ancient Strength, this phase focuses on deep, enduring tones like Deep Forest Green that anchor the visual landscape.

Harmony

Representing Composed Calm, this phase explores balanced, restorative mid-tones like Moss Green that symbolize organic resilience.

Growth

Representing Vital Expansion, this phase highlights high-energy shades like Fresh Spring Green that capture the forward momentum of a forest in bloom.

Why not here

This site is built around attention, the slow kind. Each color is meant to be held, read, and considered. Advertising operates on the opposite principle: interruption, urgency, the engineered feeling that something else is always more important than what you came for. Placing ads here wouldn't just be aesthetically mismatched. It would undermine the entire purpose of the work.

There is also something specific about the subject matter. A site exploring the beauty of natural color, the depth of forest greens, the calm of moss, the energy of new growth, would be particularly diminished by ads that monetize the destruction of those same things. The satire writes itself.

When ads work

Advertising is not inherently wrong. At its best, it connects people to things they genuinely need, funds work that would otherwise not exist, and can even be its own form of craft. A well-placed, contextually relevant ad on a site built around that kind of exchange, news, tools, communities, is a fair trade. The problem is not ads. It is the volume, the irrelevance, and the assumption that a visitor's attention is owed rather than earned.

Targeting and the illusion of relevance

Platforms like Google and Facebook have built their businesses on the idea that a personalized ad is a better ad. By tracking search history, browsing behavior, location, and purchases across millions of sites, they build a detailed picture of who you are and what you might buy next. The ad you see is not random. It is the result of an enormous amount of data collected, usually without much notice, over a long period of time.

There is something to the argument that a relevant ad is less of an intrusion than an irrelevant one. Seeing something you were already considering is less jarring than something you have no use for. But relevance built on surveillance is a different thing from relevance built on context. A hardware store placing an ad on a home improvement site understands its audience. An algorithm surfacing the same product because it quietly followed you across the internet for three weeks is a different kind of knowing, and most people, if they thought about it, would find it strange.
